Part 1 ended with a promise: Tamil letters next. த மி ழ் — the three syllabic units that spell தமிழ், the word for the Tamil language itself — cast as shadows on three perpendicular walls from a single 3D voxel sculpture. What sounded like a natural sequel turned out to be a fundamentally harder problem, one that forced a different kind of collaboration between human and machine.

With RAG, the constraint engine had run on autopilot. Design three Latin bitmaps on a 9×9 grid, intersect, fix the one gap, done. Latin letters are thick. They fill space generously. Their strokes overlap naturally across axes. Tamil is a different animal entirely.

◆ ◆ ◆

The Curve Problem

Tamil script is built from curves. Where Latin has posts and crossbars — the vertical stroke of an R, the horizontal bar of an A — Tamil letters flow. த has a looping head that curls into a descending tail. ம is an open arch resting on two feet. ழ is perhaps the most complex character in any Indian script — a loop, a tail, and the pulli dot of ழ் floating above like punctuation from another dimension.

Curves are the enemy of constraint satisfaction on a pixel grid. A one-pixel-wide curve through a 15×15 or 19×19 grid gives you maybe 15-20% fill rate. The RAG letters, by contrast, sat at 55%. Fill rate matters because the three-way AND operation is ruthlessly multiplicative: if letter A has 20% fill and letter B has 20% fill, the probability that a random voxel coordinate satisfies both is roughly 4%. Add a third letter at 20% and you're at 0.8%. The sculpture evaporates.

The core constraint
voxel(x, y, z) = THA[N-1-y][x] AND MI[N-1-y][z] AND ZHA[z][x]

A voxel exists only if all three bitmaps agree. Front view projects த, right view projects மி, top view projects ழ்.

With ~40% fill per letter, only ~6.4% of the N³ grid survives the three-way AND.

The first attempt used 15×15 grids with curves traced faithfully from the script. Claude designed the bitmaps, ran the constraint intersection, and reported: 29 missing shadow pixels. The letters were fighting each other in the shared space. The root cause was familiar from Part 1 — ழ's narrow tail had no overlap with த's middle section at the z-values where மி needed support — but the scale was different. This wasn't one gap in one letter. It was systemic.

After patching, we got the missing count to zero. But when I looked at the actual bitmaps on screen, the letters were unrecognizable. They looked like generic swooshes. The 15×15 grid simply wasn't enough resolution to carry the identity of Tamil letterforms once the constraint engine had chewed through them.

RAVI

The letter formations are not clear. Can you print each bitmap on a 15×15 grid so I can verify they look like the letters you intended?

They didn't. And that's when I suggested the key design shift:

RAVI

19×19 or even 24×24 may work. You could even stylize curves into traces of rectangular lines. And skip extra twists.

◆ ◆ ◆

Rectangularizing the Curves

This was the creative breakthrough, and it was a human one. The AI had been faithfully tracing curves on a pixel grid — the natural approach, the one that respects the script's calligraphic DNA. But curves on a coarse grid are lossy in exactly the wrong way for constraint satisfaction: thin, meandering, unpredictable in which columns and rows they occupy.

The alternative: redesign the letters in a geometric, rectangular style. Not pixel-traced curves but architectural forms — thick horizontal and vertical bars, right-angle joins, blocky serifs. Think of it as Tamil letterforms reimagined by a Bauhaus typographer. The letter identity comes from topology — which bars connect where, which spaces open up — rather than from the specific curvature of each stroke.

The constraint engine doesn't care about calligraphy. It cares about overlap. Thick rectangular strokes give it what it needs: broad ranges of x, y, and z where all three letters can coexist.

This is where the collaboration structure inverted. In Part 1, Claude did everything: designed the bitmaps, built the renderer, diagnosed the bug, derived the fix. The human contribution was pointing at the screen and saying "that's wrong." With Tamil, the human had to become the designer.

◆ ◆ ◆

The Bitmap Editor

To enable this, Claude built a tool: a browser-based bitmap editor with three 19×19 grids side by side, one for each letter. Click and drag to paint. Right-click to erase. Buttons for shift, mirror, thicken, invert. And critically: a "Verify" button that runs the full three-way constraint check in real time and shows the shadow projections with color-coded overlays — gold for correct pixels, red for missing ones.

The workflow became a loop. I'd draw a letter shape on the grid, hit Verify, see a spray of red pixels where the constraints failed, adjust the stroke widths or positions, verify again. It was like sculpting in two dimensions while a three-dimensional truth kept correcting you.

The first few attempts were humbling. I'd design a beautiful த on the grid, thicken it once for good measure, and hit Verify to find 42 missing pixels. The culprit was always the same: my letter occupied columns or rows that the other two letters didn't reach. ழ் lives in columns 4-15. If I drew த extending out to columns 2-3 or 16, those pixels had zero z-axis support. They were structurally impossible.

CLAUDE

ZHA has no pixels in columns 0-3 or 16-18 — so any THA pixel at x=2, 3, or 16 had zero z-axis support. THA row 0 is completely empty, so MI's top hook has no x-axis partner. And THA row 17 has a single lonely pixel at column 3.

Claude's role shifted too. Instead of designing letters, it became the constraint diagnostician. I'd paste my bitmap arrays, and within seconds Claude would report which pixels were missing, why they were missing, and what the minimal fix would be. The analysis was always structural: "MI row 0 at z=12 needs an x where both THA and ZHA agree, but THA row 0 is empty — clear MI row 0 or fill THA row 0."

◆ ◆ ◆

The Three Constraints as Collaborators

Working through five or six iterations, I began to internalize something about this problem that no amount of explanation had conveyed. The three letters are not independent designs. They are not even designs with constraints. They are one design that happens to project three different shapes.

Every pixel you place in one bitmap makes a promise to the other two. When you fill row 6, column 10 of த, you're saying: "there exists at least one z-value where மி at row 6 and ழ் at column 10 are both filled." If no such z exists, your pixel becomes a lie — a red dot in the verification overlay, a shadow that the sculpture cannot cast.

Every pixel you place makes a promise to the other two letters. The sculpture is the set of promises all three can keep simultaneously.

The fill rates tell the story of convergence. My first thin-stroke designs came in at 15-19% fill — far too thin. After rectangularizing and thickening, the final designs settled at 38-42%, just under the ~50% sweet spot. Not because I aimed for that number, but because that's where the letters stabilized — thick enough for constraint compatibility, thin enough to still look like த, மி, and ழ்.

What the Machine Can't Draw

The most interesting observation from this project is about the division of labor. Claude could do everything in Part 1 because Latin letters are simple enough to design algorithmically. Thick strokes, predictable topology, high fill rates. The AI's "compressed deliberation" was sufficient for both the design and the debugging.

Tamil broke that. The letters required cultural knowledge — knowing which strokes carry the identity of த versus ட, knowing that the pulli dot on ழ் isn't decorative but phonemic, knowing that மி is a compound of ம and the vowel marker ி and both parts must be visible. Claude could approximate this from training data, but the first attempt proved the approximation wasn't enough. The 15×15 bitmaps were technically valid Tamil characters that no Tamil reader would recognize.

The human brought the thing the machine lacked: the reader's eye. I know what த looks like not because I've been trained on Unicode charts but because I grew up reading it. That perceptual ground truth — "this does or does not look like the letter" — could not be delegated.

But the structural reasoning couldn't be delegated to the human either. When I drew a letter that looked right but had 42 missing shadow pixels, I had no intuition for why. The constraint chain — "pixel (6,3) fails because MI row 6 only has z-values [2,3,4,8,9,10,14,15] and ZHA column 3 has no z-values at all" — requires tracking overlaps across three 19×19 matrices simultaneously. That's where Claude's constraint tracing was indispensable.

CLAUDE

Your new designs pass all constraints with no fixes needed.

When that message appeared on the sixth iteration, I felt something I hadn't expected. Not the satisfaction of a solved puzzle — the constraint engine had been "solved" since Part 1. It was the satisfaction of a designed thing. These letters were mine. They had the specific rectangular geometry I chose, the particular way I balanced the loop of ழ against the pillars of ம, the exact weight of த's descending stroke. Claude verified the physics. I designed the aesthetics. Neither could have done the other's job.

A Different Strange Loop

Part 1's strange loop was internal to the AI: Claude's debugging of the GEB sculpture revealed something about its own reasoning, and its description of that reasoning ("compressed deliberation") was itself an instance of the phenomenon. Meta-cognition spiraling inward.

Part 2's strange loop is between human and machine. I designed letters but couldn't verify them. Claude verified constraints but couldn't design the letters. The sculpture exists in the space between those two capabilities — a space that neither participant could occupy alone.

And the sculpture itself is about this entanglement. தமிழ் means "Tamil" — a language, a culture, a way of seeing. The word can only appear as three shadows cast by one object, and that object can only exist when all three projections agree. No letter stands alone. No shadow makes sense without the others. The word Tamil, spelled in Tamil, carved into the constraints of three-dimensional space.

Hofstadter's original GEB cover was a metaphor for the book's thesis: that Gödel's incompleteness, Escher's impossible geometries, and Bach's self-referential canons are all instances of the same deep pattern. Our Tamil version carries a different thesis: that the most interesting creative artifacts emerge not from a single intelligence but from the entanglement of complementary ones. The human brings cultural intuition. The machine brings constraint reasoning. The sculpture is what neither could build alone.

The word Tamil, spelled in Tamil, carved into the constraints of three-dimensional space — where no letter stands alone and no shadow makes sense without the others.

991 voxels. Three perfect shadows. Six iterations of a human-machine design loop. And one wooden-colored sculpture spinning in a browser window, casting letters from a language that's been spoken for two thousand years onto walls made of WebGL.

The math scales. The beauty compounds. And the debugging teaches us something new each time — not just about the sculpture, but about the sculptors.
